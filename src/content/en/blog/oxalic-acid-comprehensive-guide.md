### Industrial Applications and Mechanisms of Oxalic Acid

With its unique physicochemical properties, oxalic acid demonstrates widespread application value and mechanisms across multiple industrial sectors.

#### 1. Textile Dyeing and Printing Industry

*   **Bleaching and Purification**: Oxalic acid effectively removes pigments and impurities from cellulosic fibers, enhancing their whiteness and luster [1]. Its primary mechanism involves the complexation of metal ions (e.g., iron ions) from water or raw materials, preventing them from catalyzing the decomposition of bleaching agents or causing yellowing, thereby improving bleaching efficacy [1]. For example, it forms soluble iron(III) oxalate anionic complexes with ferric ions, effectively removing rust stains from fabrics [1].
*   **Mordanting**: In the dyeing process, oxalic acid can help certain dyes bind to fibers, improving colorfastness [1].
*   **pH Regulation**: Oxalic acid is used to adjust the pH of dye baths, creating a more stable or more receptive acidic environment for specific dyes, thereby improving dyeing uniformity [1].
*   **Reducing Agent**: In some dyeing and printing processes, such as the development of indigo dyes, oxalic acid can act as a reducing agent to eliminate nitrous acid, preventing reddish-brown precipitates or off-white backgrounds on fabrics [1]. In discharge printing, it also helps remove ground colors [1].
*   **Water Softening and Post-treatment**: Oxalic acid can complex with hard water ions like calcium and magnesium, preventing them from forming precipitates with dyes, thereby improving dyeing effects and fabric feel [1]. In fabric post-treatment, oxalic acid can be used to remove residual sizing agents and auxiliaries from surfaces, making the fabric smoother and brighter [1].

#### 2. Metal Treatment Industry

*   **Rust Removal**: Oxalic acid is an ideal rust remover. It reacts chemically with rust (iron oxide) to form soluble iron oxalate complexes, which can be easily removed from metal surfaces [1]. This mechanism makes old tools look new and is widely used in the maintenance of infrastructure, shipbuilding, and heavy machinery.
*   **Surface Treatment and Polishing**: Oxalic acid is used for the precision surface treatment of metals, polishing of aluminum products, and marble polishing [1]. In electroplating, oxalic acid can act as a complexing agent or pH regulator, helping to form smooth surfaces and improve plating uniformity, especially in the electrolytic polishing of aluminum or copper [1].
*   **Emerging Applications: Electronics Manufacturing**: In semiconductor manufacturing and display manufacturing, high-purity oxalic acid is used for wafer cleaning, oxide removal, and precision surface treatment [2].

#### 3. Rare Earth Separation and Extraction

*   **Selective Precipitation**: Rare earth elements usually exist in mineral forms. In the rare earth extraction process, oxalic acid combines with trivalent rare earth ions (RE³⁺) to form water-insoluble and slightly acid-soluble rare earth oxalate precipitates (RE₂(C₂O₄)₃·nH₂O) [1]. By adjusting the solution's pH (typically between 1-3) and oxalic acid concentration, highly selective separation of rare earths from non-rare earth impurities like iron and aluminum can be achieved, as these impurities form soluble complexes at low pH and remain in solution [1].
*   **Leaching**: Oxalic acid can also be used as a leaching agent, reacting with metal ions in rare earth minerals to form soluble oxalate complexes, thereby dissolving rare earth elements [1].
*   **Purification**: The precipitated rare earth oxalate, after filtration and washing, is calcined at high temperatures (800-1000℃) to decompose into high-purity rare earth oxides (REO) [1].
*   **Process Optimization**: Changing from direct addition of solid oxalic acid to adding oxalic acid solution for precipitation can reduce oxalic acid consumption and improve economic efficiency [1]. Oxalic acid concentration, reaction temperature, time, and pH are critical parameters affecting the leaching rate and selectivity of rare earths, requiring optimization [1].
*   **Wastewater Treatment and Resource Recovery**: Wastewater from rare earth oxalate precipitation contains small amounts of oxalic acid and hydrochloric acid, and direct discharge would cause serious environmental pollution. By counter-current extraction of oxalic acid and hydrochloric acid from wastewater using inert organic extractants (e.g., N235), an organic phase loaded with acids is obtained, which is then back-extracted with deionized water to obtain a concentrated mixed acid solution. Subsequently, another extractant (e.g., TBP) is used to separate oxalic acid and hydrochloric acid, recovering oxalic acid solution and hydrochloric acid solution separately. This method achieves zero wastewater discharge and recovery of valuable resources. Furthermore, in the pretreatment of rare earth smelting wastewater, sulfuric acid replaces calcium oxalate, converting it into oxalic acid for recycling, which significantly reduces oxalic acid consumption and wastewater treatment costs, with oxalic acid recovery rates exceeding 90%.

#### 4. Pharmaceutical Intermediates

Oxalic acid is a key intermediate and purification agent in drug manufacturing. It is used in the production of various drugs, such as tetracycline, oxytetracycline, phenobarbital, and borneol, and for the purification of compounds. The pharmaceutical industry has extremely high purity requirements for oxalic acid; pharmaceutical-grade oxalic acid is continuously being enhanced to meet increasingly stringent regulatory standards, ensuring the safety and effectiveness of drug synthesis.

#### 5. Cleaning Agents

*   **Mechanism**: It primarily utilizes its acidity to react with metal oxides, forming soluble substances to remove stains and rust. For example, it can easily dissolve yellow stains in kitchen stainless steel sinks, black grime on bathroom tiles, and toilet limescale [1].
*   **Precautions**: Due to its acidity, oxalic acid is corrosive to marble and calcareous materials and must be used with caution [1].

#### 6. Agricultural Sector

*   **Pest Control**: Widely used in beekeeping and crop protection for the control of mites and pests [2].
*   **Environmental Remediation**: Oxalic acid is also used for the remediation of heavy metal contamination in agricultural soils. Research shows that oxalic acid has good removal efficiency for arsenic (As), with elution rates reaching over 90% [3].

#### 7. Emerging Industrial Applications

The application of oxalic acid in emerging industries is growing, particularly in new energy and new materials fields.
*   **New Energy**:

## Chapter 2: Oxalic Acid Production Processes, Safety Management, and Environmental Strategies

Oxalic acid (ethanedioic acid, chemical formula H₂C₂O₄) is the simplest dicarboxylic acid and an important organic chemical raw material, widely used in various industries such as dyes, bleaching, rust removal, chemical synthesis, catalysts, pharmaceuticals, and printing and dyeing. ### 2.1 Mainstream Oxalic Acid Production Processes

The industrial production methods for oxalic acid are diverse, primarily including the sodium formate method, oxidation method, carbonyl synthesis method, ethylene glycol oxidation method, propylene oxidation method, and emerging electrochemical synthesis methods. Each of these processes has its own characteristics.

#### 2.1.1 Sodium Formate Method

The sodium formate method is a traditional process. It involves the reaction of carbon monoxide with sodium hydroxide under pressure to produce sodium formate, which is then dehydrogenated at high temperatures to form sodium oxalate, and finally acidified, crystallized, and dehydrated to obtain oxalic acid product.

*   **Process Flow**:
    *   Purified carbon monoxide reacts with sodium hydroxide under pressure (usually 1.8-2.0 MPa) to produce sodium formate.
    *   Sodium formate is then heated to 360-400°C for dehydrogenation, forming sodium oxalate.
    *   Sodium oxalate is converted to oxalic acid by acidification with sulfuric acid, followed by cooling, crystallization, and purification.
*   **Pros and Cons Analysis**: This method is mature and the raw materials are readily available. However, it typically involves high energy consumption and produces a significant amount of by-product sodium sulfate, leading to waste disposal challenges.

#### 2.1.2 Oxidation Method

The oxidation method primarily uses starch, glucose, or carbohydrates as raw materials, undergoing an oxidation reaction in the presence of a catalyst.

*   **Process Flow**:
    *   Using starch or glucose mother liquor as raw materials, oxalic acid is produced through an oxidation reaction with a mixed nitric acid-sulfuric acid solution in the presence of catalysts such as vanadium pentoxide.
    *   Nitrogen oxides in the exhaust gas are typically recovered and recycled.
*   **Pros and Cons Analysis**: This method is generally effective for producing oxalic acid but involves the use of strong oxidizing agents (nitric acid), which can generate environmentally hazardous nitrogen oxide emissions. It also faces challenges in achieving high selectivity and minimizing by-products.

#### 2.1.3 Carbonyl Synthesis Method

The carbonyl synthesis method is one of the more commonly adopted oxalic acid production methods. This method involves the reaction of carbon monoxide with alcohols (such as n-butanol) to produce oxalic esters (e.g., dibutyl oxalate), which are then hydrolyzed to obtain oxalic acid.

*   **Process Flow**:
    *   High-purity (over 90%) carbon monoxide reacts with n-butanol in the presence of a palladium catalyst to form dibutyl oxalate.
    *   Dibutyl oxalate is then hydrolyzed to produce oxalic acid and n-butanol, with n-butanol being recycled.
*   **Pros and Cons Analysis**: This method offers advantages such as a clean process, high conversion rate, and good selectivity. It is considered a more environmentally friendly approach compared to traditional oxidation methods, especially with advanced catalytic systems.

#### 2.1.4 Ethylene Glycol Oxidation Method

The ethylene glycol oxidation method involves producing oxalic acid by air oxidation of ethylene glycol in the presence of nitric acid and sulfuric acid.

*   **Pros and Cons Analysis**:
    *   **Advantages**: Clear raw material source and relatively easy to control.
    *   **Disadvantages**: This method is energy-intensive and causes significant pollution, thus it has gradually been phased out in modern industrial production.

#### 2.1.5 Propylene Oxidation Method

The propylene oxidation method proceeds in two steps:

*   **Process Flow**:
    *   **Step 1**: Propylene is oxidized with nitric acid to convert it into α-nitrolactic acid.
    *   **Step 2**: The α-nitrolactic acid is further catalytically oxidized, or mixed acids are used as oxidizing agents, to ultimately obtain oxalic acid.
*   **Pros and Cons Analysis**: This method requires complex separation and purification steps, and may generate by-products that are difficult to handle, limiting its widespread application.

#### 2.1.6 Electrochemical Method (Emerging/Experimental)

The electrochemical method is a new, clean, and efficient process for synthesizing oxalic acid by electrochemical oxidation of acetylene.

*   **Process Flow**:
    *   In experiments, a platinum electrode is used as the anode and a palladium electrode as the cathode. Cyclic voltammetry is employed to study the electrocatalytic oxidation process of acetylene.
    *   Acetylene is oxidized to oxalic acid on the platinum catalyst in the anodic region. The cathode typically uses a sulfuric acid solution containing a certain volume fraction of acetone as the supporting electrolyte, while the anode also contains acetone.
*   **Pros and Cons Analysis**: As an emerging technology, the electrochemical method offers advantages such as environmental friendliness and high energy efficiency. However, it is still largely in the experimental or pilot-plant stage, facing challenges in scale-up, electrode material stability, and process optimization for industrial application.

### 2.2 Toxicological Characteristics and Occupational Exposure Risks of Oxalic Acid

Oxalic acid, as an important organic chemical raw material, necessitates high attention to its significant toxicological characteristics during its production and application. Comprehensive understanding of its hazards is crucial for establishing effective safety management and environmental protection strategies [1].

#### 2.2.1 Acute Toxicity

*   **Lethal Dose**: For adults, the minimum lethal dose of oxalic acid is approximately 71 mg/kg body weight [1]. Ingestion of 2-5 grams of oxalic acid can potentially be fatal [1].
*   **Animal Studies**: Studies in rats showed an oral LD50 of 7500 mg/kg [1]. For mice, the intraperitoneal LD50 was 270 mg/kg [1]. In dogs, the lowest published lethal dose (LDLo) for oral ingestion was 1000 mg/kg [1].

#### 2.2.2 Irritancy and Corrosivity

*   **Skin Contact**: Direct contact with oxalic acid can cause severe burns [1]. Prolonged contact with a 5%-10% oxalic acid solution may lead to numbness of the fingers, cyanosis, yellowing of nails, and even gangrene [1]. Experimental studies showed mild irritation after 24 hours of skin contact with 500 mg of oxalic acid in rabbits [1].
*   **Eye Contact**: Oxalic acid is harmful to the eyes, capable of causing severe irritation [1]. Experimental results from 24 hours of eye contact with 0.25 mg of oxalic acid in rabbits showed severe irritation [1].
*   **Digestive Tract**: If accidentally ingested, oxalic acid will corrode the digestive tract and strongly irritate the gastrointestinal mucosa, leading to symptoms such as abdominal pain, diarrhea, nausea, and vomiting [1].

#### 2.2.3 Symptoms of Poisoning and Physiological Mechanisms

*   **Digestive System**: After oral ingestion of oxalic acid, patients will immediately feel burning and pain in the mouth, throat, and behind the sternum, accompanied by dysphagia, dry mouth, thirst, frequent vomiting (vomitus may sometimes be bloody), severe upper abdominal pain, and bloody stools [1].
*   **Respiratory System**: Inhalation of oxalic acid vapor or dust can cause irritation of the respiratory tract mucosa [1]. High concentrations of acidic volatile substances can also cause burns to the respiratory tract, leading to coughing and sputum production [1].
*   **Urinary System**: Oxalic acid readily combines with calcium ions in the human body to form insoluble calcium oxalate [1], which in turn leads to hypocalcemia [1]. Manifestations of hypocalcemia include numbness and pain in the limbs, locked jaw, muscle twitching, ankle clonus, convulsions, and even coma [1]. If calcium oxalate crystals deposit in the renal tubules, it can cause acute kidney failure [1], leading to oliguria, anuria, and even the severe consequence of uremia [1], with oxalic acid crystals detectable in the urine [1]. Furthermore, acute kidney injury (AKI) caused by dimethyl oxalate poisoning is another important feature, with renal biopsy results typically showing acute tubular interstitial disease accompanied by massive oxalate deposition [1].
*   **Nervous System**: Poisoning can cause arrhythmia and central nervous system depression [1]. Prolonged exposure to oxalic acid in children may even affect brain development, leading to intellectual disability [1].
*   **Physiological Mechanisms**: Oxalic acid is a potent chelating agent [1] that can combine with calcium ions to form complexes with higher bioavailability [1]. Additionally, oxalic acid may cause mitochondrial dysfunction [1]. In cases of ethylene glycol poisoning, the metabolic product of ethylene glycol in the body is oxalic acid, which is one of the key factors leading to acute kidney failure [1].

#### 2.2.4 Occupational Exposure Limits and Risk Assessment

To ensure the health and safety of workers, countries and regions worldwide have established strict relevant limits and risk assessment guidelines for occupational exposure to oxalic acid.

*   **OSHA Permissible Exposure Limit (PEL)**: The threshold limit value for ethanedioic acid in workplace air is 1 mg/m³ (TLV-TWA, USA, 1980) [1].
*   **Hazard Identification**: This involves collecting data on production processes, protective measures, properties of chemical toxins, and occupational exposure situations to identify characteristics such as acute toxicity, irritation, corrosivity, and carcinogenicity [1].
*   **Risk Assessment**: Quantifying the probability and severity of adverse health effects under specific exposure conditions, and proposing control measures [1].

### 2.3 Safe Storage, Transportation, and Emergency Response of Oxalic Acid

Given the strong corrosivity and toxicity of oxalic acid, strict safety guidelines must be followed for its storage, transportation, and emergency response to minimize potential hazards.

#### 2.3.1 Storage and Transportation

*   **Storage Environment**: Oxalic acid must be stored away from moisture and rain, and isolated from oxidizing agents and alkaline substances to prevent dangerous reactions. The storage location should be far from residential areas and food to ensure safety.
*   **Transportation**: When loading oxalic acid onto ships, it should not be placed on the deck but rather in well-protected holds. During road and rail transport, it should be packaged in sealed containers, clearly labeled, and secured to prevent spillage or damage.

#### 2.3.2 Personal Protection

*   **Protective Equipment**: Operators handling oxalic acid must wear professional protective gloves, safety goggles, face shields, and respirators, along with other personal protective equipment.

#### 2.3.3 Emergency Response

*   **Contact Treatment**: If skin or eyes accidentally come into contact with oxalic acid, immediately flush the affected area with large amounts of water for at least 15 minutes and seek medical attention as soon as possible.
*   **Ingestion Treatment**: In the event of accidental ingestion of oxalic acid, the patient must be immediately taken to a hospital for treatment. For those who have ingested it, gastric lavage with calcium-containing substances such as calcium lactate, calcium gluconate, dilute limewater, chalk powder, or milk can be considered to precipitate ethanedioic acid. However, gastric lavage must be performed very cautiously [1]. Intravenous injection of 10 ml of 10% calcium gluconate can serve as an effective antidote, and it can be repeated if necessary [1]. At the same time, attention should be paid to correcting acidosis and actively preventing potential acute kidney failure [1].
*   **Fire Treatment**: Although oxalic acid itself is not easily combustible, if a fire occurs due to other reasons, water and sand can be used to extinguish it. Carbon dioxide or dry chemical extinguishers are also suitable for small fires.
*   **Pollution Disposal**: A dedicated neutralization agent spraying plan for oxalic acid pollution should be formulated in advance, allowing for rapid and effective treatment in case of a spill [1]. Spills should be contained, absorbed with inert material, and disposed of according to local regulations.

### 2.4 Environmental Impact of Oxalic Acid Production and Use and Wastewater Treatment

Wastewater generated during the production and use of oxalic acid, if discharged directly without effective treatment, will cause severe environmental pollution. Addressing these environmental concerns is paramount for sustainable industrial development.

#### 2.4.1 Environmental Impact

*   **Water Pollution**: Oxalic acid wastewater typically exhibits strong acidity (low pH), contains high concentrations of organic matter (indicated by high Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD) and high Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD)), and has a complex composition, with some organic matter being difficult to biodegrade. Direct discharge will lead to water acidification, significant depletion of dissolved oxygen in the water, severe harm to aquatic life, and destruction of the entire aquatic ecosystem.
*   **Secondary Pollution**: When oxalic acid is used as a pH regulator, excessive use or reaction products with other substances may cause secondary pollution to the environment. For instance, complexation with heavy metals can lead to novel contaminants.
*   **Bioaccumulation in the Food Chain**: Oxalic acid and its derivatives may enter the environment through atmospheric diffusion, soil adsorption, and other pathways. Furthermore, complexes formed between oxalic acid and heavy metals may undergo bioaccumulation effects through aquatic food chains, leading to heavy metal enrichment in crops, posing potential threats to ecosystems and human health [1].

#### 2.4.2 Wastewater Sources and Characteristics

*   **Oxalic Acid Mother Liquor**: In oxidation methods and other processes, after a certain number of cycles, oxalic acid mother liquor cannot be reused due to excessive accumulation of impurities, thus forming black-colored mixed waste liquid that may contain oily substances or suspended particles.
*   **Industrial Wastewater**: Industrial wastewater from chemical, pharmaceutical, food processing, textile dyeing, and other industries usually contains high concentrations of oxalic acid, and may be accompanied by other unreacted raw materials, intermediate products, sugars, proteins, amino acids, inorganic salts, and other complex components. The precise composition varies significantly depending on the specific application.

#### 2.4.3 Wastewater Treatment Technologies

Given the complexity and pollution characteristics of oxalic acid wastewater, its treatment requires the comprehensive application of multiple technologies, typically including pretreatment, biological treatment, and advanced treatment.

*   **Pretreatment**:
    *   **Physical Separation**: Facilities such as grilles, grit chambers, and sedimentation tanks are used to effectively remove suspended solids and large particulate impurities.
    *   **Chemical Precipitation**: Chemical agents are added to promote the precipitation of insoluble substances, especially metal ions.
    *   **Coagulation-Flocculation**: Coagulants and flocculants are used to aggregate fine suspended particles and colloidal substances into larger flocs for easier removal.
    *   **Adjust pH**: Regulating the pH of the wastewater to optimize subsequent treatment processes.
*   **Biological Treatment**:
    *   **Aerobic Processes**: Activated sludge processes (e.g., A/O, A2/O), biological contact oxidation, and biofilm reactors are used to biodegrade organic pollutants under aerobic conditions.
    *   **Anaerobic Processes**: Upflow anaerobic sludge blanket (UASB) reactors are effective for treating high-concentration organic wastewater, converting organic matter into biogas.
*   **Advanced Treatment**:
    *   **Membrane Technology**: Ultrafiltration, nanofiltration, and reverse osmosis can remove residual suspended solids, dissolved organic matter, and inorganic salts, achieving high-quality effluent.
    *   **Adsorption**: Activated carbon adsorption can effectively remove refractory organic pollutants and color from wastewater.
    *   **Advanced Oxidation Processes (AOPs)**: Technologies such as Fenton oxidation, ozone oxidation, and UV/H₂O₂ can break down complex organic molecules that are difficult to biodegrade.

#### 2.4.4 Environmental Regulations and Emission Standards

*   **National Standards**: For example, the "Environmental Protection Law of the People's Republic of China" and the "Water Pollution Prevention and Control Law of the People's Republic of China" provide the overall legal framework for wastewater discharge. Internationally, regulations like those set by the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) or the European Union's industrial emissions directive also provide benchmarks.
*   **Industry Standards**: Industry-specific standards such as wastewater discharge standards for the pharmaceutical industry (especially extraction categories) put forward specific requirements for wastewater discharge from relevant enterprises.
*   **Local Standards**: Different regions may formulate more stringent emission standards based on local environmental characteristics and protection needs.
*   **Integrated Emission Standards**: The "Integrated Wastewater Discharge Standard" (GB 8978–1996) is applicable to pharmaceutical industrial wastewater, but this standard is less targeted and does not specify the discharge volume per unit product. Future trends lean towards more specific and stringent industry-specific standards and performance-based regulations.
