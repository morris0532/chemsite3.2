In the rigorous and precision-driven field of analytical chemistry, the accurate determination of substance concentrations is paramount for quality control, research, and regulatory compliance. Among the myriad of volumetric analysis techniques, **iodometric titration** stands as one of the most widely employed and reliable methods, particularly for quantifying oxidizing agents. At the heart of this technique lies **Sodium Thiosulphate (Na₂S₂O₃)**, an indispensable reducing agent that serves as the primary titrant. Its ability to react quantitatively and rapidly with iodine makes it a cornerstone for a diverse range of quantitative analyses, from measuring dissolved oxygen in environmental water samples to assessing the purity of industrial chemicals and pharmaceutical compounds [1].

This comprehensive guide provides a deep technical dive into the role of Sodium Thiosulphate in analytical chemistry. We will explore the fundamental principles of iodometric titration, detail the meticulous procedures for preparing and standardizing Sodium Thiosulphate solutions, discuss its critical applications across various industries, and highlight the essential factors that influence the accuracy and stability of this vital reagent. For laboratory managers, analytical chemists, quality assurance professionals, and students of chemistry, a thorough understanding of Sodium Thiosulphate and iodometric titration is fundamental for achieving precise and reproducible analytical results.

## The Fundamental Principles of Iodometric Titration

Iodometric titration is a type of redox (reduction-oxidation) titration. It is an indirect method, meaning that the analyte (the substance being analyzed) does not react directly with the titrant (Sodium Thiosulphate). Instead, the analyte first reacts with an excess of iodide ions (I⁻) to liberate a proportional amount of elementary iodine (I₂). This liberated iodine is then quantitatively titrated with a standard solution of Sodium Thiosulphate [2].

### The Two-Step Process:

1.  **Liberation of Iodine**: An oxidizing agent (the analyte) reacts with an excess of iodide ions in an acidic medium to produce iodine. The amount of iodine liberated is directly proportional to the amount of oxidizing agent present.
    `Oxidizing Agent + 2I⁻ → Reduced Product + I₂`

2.  **Titration of Liberated Iodine**: The liberated iodine is then titrated with a standard solution of Sodium Thiosulphate. The reaction proceeds as follows:
    `I₂ + 2S₂O₃²⁻ → 2I⁻ + S₄O₆²⁻`

In this second reaction, iodine (I₂) is reduced to iodide ions (I⁻), while the thiosulphate ion (S₂O₃²⁻) is oxidized to the tetrathionate ion (S₄O₆²⁻). The reaction is highly specific, rapid, and proceeds smoothly in neutral or slightly acidic solutions. The endpoint is typically detected using a starch indicator, which forms a deep blue complex with iodine.

### Why Iodometry is Preferred:

*   **Versatility**: Applicable to a wide range of oxidizing agents.
*   **Accuracy**: The reactions are quantitative and well-understood.
*   **Visual Endpoint**: The starch-iodine complex provides a sharp and easily detectable color change.
*   **Cost-Effective**: Reagents are relatively inexpensive.

## 1. Preparing and Standardizing a Standard Sodium Thiosulphate Solution

Unlike primary standards (substances of high purity, known composition, and stability that can be weighed directly to prepare a solution of exact concentration), Sodium Thiosulphate Pentahydrate (Na₂S₂O₃·5H₂O) is a **secondary standard**. This is because it is not perfectly stable; it can slowly decompose, effloresce (lose water of hydration), or absorb moisture and carbon dioxide from the air. Therefore, a solution of approximate concentration must first be prepared and then accurately standardized against a primary standard [3].

### 1.1 Preparation Steps for an Approximately 0.1 N (0.05 M) Solution:

1.  **Water Preparation**: Use distilled or deionized water that has been boiled for 10-15 minutes and then cooled to room temperature. Boiling removes dissolved carbon dioxide (CO₂), which can react with thiosulphate to form sulfurous acid, leading to decomposition. It also sterilizes the water, preventing the growth of sulfur-metabolizing bacteria that can consume thiosulphate.
2.  **Weighing**: Accurately weigh approximately 24.82 grams of Sodium Thiosulphate Pentahydrate (for a 0.1 N solution, as its equivalent weight is 248.2 g/mol) for every liter of solution required. For a 0.05 M solution, this would be approximately 12.41 grams.
3.  **Dissolution**: Dissolve the weighed Sodium Thiosulphate in the prepared (boiled and cooled) distilled water in a volumetric flask. Make up to the mark with the same water.
4.  **Stabilization**: Add a small amount (e.g., 0.1g) of sodium carbonate (Na₂CO₃) or borax to the solution. This helps to maintain a slightly alkaline pH (around 9-10), which significantly enhances the long-term stability of the thiosulphate solution by inhibiting bacterial growth and acid-catalyzed decomposition.
5.  **Storage**: Store the prepared solution in a clean, dark, stoppered amber glass bottle to protect it from light and air. Allow the solution to stand for at least 24 hours before standardization to ensure any initial decomposition is complete.

### 1.2 Standardization Against a Primary Standard:

The exact concentration of the prepared Sodium Thiosulphate solution must be determined by titrating it against a known primary standard. Common primary standards for iodometric titrations include:

*   **Potassium Iodate (KIO₃)**: This is a highly stable, non-hygroscopic primary standard. It reacts with excess iodide in an acidic medium to liberate a precise amount of iodine.
    `IO₃⁻ + 5I⁻ + 6H⁺ → 3I₂ + 3H₂O`
*   **Potassium Dichromate (K₂Cr₂O₇)**: Another stable primary standard, though its reaction with iodide is slower and requires a higher acidity.
*   **Potassium Bromate (KBrO₃)**: Similar to iodate, it liberates iodine from iodide in acidic conditions.

**Standardization Procedure (using Potassium Iodate as an example):**

1.  **Prepare KIO₃ Standard**: Accurately weigh a precise amount of dry KIO₃ and dissolve it in a known volume of distilled water to prepare a primary standard solution of known concentration.
2.  **Reaction with Iodide**: Pipette a known volume of the KIO₃ standard solution into an Erlenmeyer flask. Add an excess of potassium iodide (KI) solution and acidify with a strong acid (e.g., HCl or H₂SO₄). This liberates iodine.
3.  **Titration**: Titrate the liberated iodine with the approximate Sodium Thiosulphate solution from a burette.
4.  **Starch Indicator**: Add a few drops of starch indicator solution when the iodine color (yellow-brown) has faded to a pale straw yellow. The solution will turn deep blue due to the formation of the starch-iodine complex. Continue titrating dropwise until the blue color suddenly disappears, leaving a colorless solution. This is the endpoint.
5.  **Calculation**: Use the volume of Sodium Thiosulphate consumed and the known concentration of the KIO₃ solution to calculate the exact molarity (or normality) of the Sodium Thiosulphate solution.

Regular restandardization (e.g., weekly or monthly) is recommended, especially for critical analyses, due to the inherent instability of thiosulphate solutions.

## 2. Key Applications in Quantitative Analysis: Diverse Industrial & Environmental Uses

Sodium Thiosulphate, through iodometric titration, finds extensive application across various scientific and industrial domains for the accurate determination of oxidizing species.

### 2.1 Dissolved Oxygen (DO) Determination: The Winkler Method

The **Winkler Method** (or iodometric method) is the standard and most accurate technique for measuring dissolved oxygen (DO) in water samples, crucial for environmental monitoring, wastewater treatment, and aquaculture. The method involves a series of reactions:

1.  **Fixation**: Manganese(II) sulfate and alkaline iodide are added to the water sample, forming manganese hydroxide. Dissolved oxygen oxidizes Mn(II) to Mn(III).
2.  **Acidification & Iodine Liberation**: Upon acidification, the Mn(III) oxidizes iodide ions to liberate iodine, proportional to the original DO concentration.
3.  **Titration**: The liberated iodine is then titrated with a standard Sodium Thiosulphate solution, with starch as the indicator. The volume of thiosulphate consumed directly correlates to the DO concentration [4].

### 2.2 Determination of Chlorine Residuals in Water

Sodium Thiosulphate is widely used to determine free and total chlorine residuals in drinking water, swimming pools, and industrial process water. This is vital for ensuring effective disinfection while avoiding excessive chlorine levels that can cause taste/odor issues or equipment corrosion. The chlorine directly oxidizes iodide to iodine, which is then titrated with thiosulphate.

### 2.3 Copper Determination in Ores and Alloys

Iodometry is a classic and reliable method for determining the copper content in various samples, including ores, alloys, and some industrial products. Copper(II) ions react with iodide to liberate iodine, which is subsequently titrated with a standard thiosulphate solution. This method is particularly useful for samples where other analytical techniques might be interfered with [5].

### 2.4 Analysis of Bleaching Powder and Oxidizing Agents

The "available chlorine" content in bleaching powder (calcium hypochlorite, Ca(OCl)₂) and other hypochlorite solutions (e.g., sodium hypochlorite) is a critical quality parameter. This is determined by reacting the hypochlorite with potassium iodide in an acidic medium to liberate iodine, which is then titrated with a standard thiosulphate solution. This ensures the bleaching agent has the desired oxidizing power for applications in textiles, paper, and sanitation.

### 2.5 Peroxide Value Determination in Fats and Oils

In food chemistry, the peroxide value (PV) is a measure of the extent of oxidative rancidity in fats and oils. The method involves reacting peroxides with iodide to liberate iodine, which is then titrated with Sodium Thiosulphate. This helps in assessing the quality and shelf life of edible oils and fat-containing products.

### 2.6 Determination of Sulfur Dioxide (SO₂) in Air Pollution Studies

Iodometric titration can be adapted to determine sulfur dioxide concentrations in air samples. SO₂ is absorbed into a solution, and then its reducing properties are used to react with iodine, or it can be oxidized to sulfate, and the excess oxidant is then back-titrated with thiosulphate.

## 3. Factors Affecting Stability and Accuracy: Best Practices for Reliable Results

To ensure the highest accuracy and precision in analytical work involving Sodium Thiosulphate, several critical factors related to its stability and reaction conditions must be carefully controlled.

### 3.1 Solution Stability and Decomposition:

Sodium Thiosulphate solutions are inherently unstable and can decompose through several pathways:

*   **Acid-Catalyzed Decomposition**: In acidic solutions (pH < 6), thiosulphate decomposes to sulfur and sulfur dioxide. This is why iodometric titrations are typically performed in neutral or slightly acidic conditions, and the addition of acid to liberate iodine should be done immediately before titration.
    `S₂O₃²⁻ + 2H⁺ → S + SO₂ + H₂O`
*   **Bacterial Action**: Certain sulfur-metabolizing bacteria, such as *Thiobacillus thioparus*, can metabolize thiosulphate, leading to a decrease in its concentration. Boiling the water during preparation and adding a preservative (like sodium carbonate) helps to mitigate this.
*   **Oxidation by Air**: Although slow, atmospheric oxygen can oxidize thiosulphate to sulfate, especially in the presence of light or trace metal catalysts.
*   **Light Sensitivity**: Exposure to direct sunlight can accelerate the decomposition of thiosulphate solutions. Therefore, storage in amber glass bottles is crucial.

### 3.2 pH Control:

The pH of the solution is critical. The titration should ideally be performed at a pH between 6 and 8. In strongly alkaline solutions, thiosulphate can be oxidized to sulfate, leading to incorrect results. In strongly acidic solutions, thiosulphate decomposes, as mentioned above.

### 3.3 Starch Indicator:

*   **Timing of Addition**: Starch indicator should only be added when the iodine color has faded to a pale straw yellow. If added too early, the starch can form an irreversible complex with high concentrations of iodine, leading to a false endpoint.
*   **Freshness**: Starch solutions are susceptible to bacterial decomposition and should be prepared fresh or preserved (e.g., with salicylic acid) and replaced regularly.
*   **Temperature**: Starch indicator is more sensitive at lower temperatures.

### 3.4 Purity of Reagents:

Using high-purity reagents, especially the primary standard for standardization, is fundamental for accurate results. Contaminants can lead to side reactions or inaccurate concentrations.
