**Sodium Thiosulphate** (Na₂S₂O₃), commonly known as sodium hyposulfite or "hypo," is a versatile inorganic compound with a wide array of applications across various industries. It typically appears as a white, translucent crystalline solid, readily soluble in water. Its unique chemical properties, particularly its reducing capabilities and ability to complex with metal ions, make it indispensable in fields ranging from photography and medicine to water treatment and chemical analysis. ## Chemical Properties and Characteristics

Sodium Thiosulphate exists in several hydrated forms, with the pentahydrate (Na₂S₂O₃·5H₂O) being the most common. It has a molecular weight of 248.18 g/mol for the pentahydrate form and a CAS number of 7772-98-7. Key properties include:

*   **Appearance**: White, translucent crystals or powder.
*   **Solubility**: Highly soluble in water, forming clear solutions.
*   **Reducing Agent**: It is a strong reducing agent, particularly in acidic solutions where it decomposes to sulfur, sulfur dioxide, and water. This property is crucial for its use as an antichlor.
*   **Complexing Agent**: It forms stable complexes with various metal ions, notably silver, which is fundamental to its photographic applications.
*   **pH**: Aqueous solutions are typically neutral to slightly alkaline.

## Diverse Applications Across Industries

The broad utility of Sodium Thiosulphate stems from its unique chemical reactivity. Its applications are categorized by the specific industries it serves:

### 1. Photography and Film Processing

Historically, Sodium Thiosulphate has been most famous for its role as a **fixing agent** in silver-halide photographic processes. After exposure to light, silver halide crystals in film or photographic paper are reduced to metallic silver. The unexposed silver halides are then removed by the thiosulphate, which forms soluble silver thiosulphate complexes, thus "fixing" the image and making it permanent and light-stable. This process prevents further darkening of the image.

### 2. Water Treatment and Environmental Applications

In water treatment, Sodium Thiosulphate acts as an **antichlor**, effectively neutralizing chlorine and chloramines in tap water, wastewater, and swimming pools. This is vital for protecting aquatic life, preventing the formation of harmful chlorinated byproducts, and preparing water for sensitive biological processes. It is also used to detoxify cyanide waste by converting it to less toxic thiocyanate.

### 3. Medical and Pharmaceutical Uses

Medically, Sodium Thiosulphate is an important antidote for **cyanide poisoning** and **arsenic poisoning**. It works by converting cyanide to thiocyanate, which is less toxic and can be excreted by the kidneys. It is also used in the treatment of calciphylaxis, a rare but severe complication of end-stage renal disease, and as an antifungal agent.

### 4. Chemical Analysis and Laboratory Reagent

In analytical chemistry, Sodium Thiosulphate is a standard reagent for **iodometric titrations**. It is used to determine the concentration of oxidizing agents (like iodine) in a sample. The reaction involves the reduction of iodine to iodide ions, with thiosulphate being oxidized to tetrathionate.

### 5. Industrial and Other Uses

*   **Textile Industry**: Used as a dechlorinating agent after bleaching and as a mordant.
*   **Leather Tanning**: Employed in the tanning process.
*   **Gold Extraction**: Can be used as an alternative lixiviant to cyanide in gold extraction, particularly in less toxic processes.
*   **Paper Industry**: Used as a reducing agent in pulp and paper production.

## Safety, Handling, and Storage

Sodium Thiosulphate is generally considered safe, but proper handling is essential. It should be stored in a cool, dry place, away from strong acids, oxidizing agents, and heat, as it can decompose to release sulfur dioxide gas. Personal protective equipment, including gloves and eye protection, should be worn during handling. Ingestion or prolonged skin contact should be avoided.
