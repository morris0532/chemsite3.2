The reaction between **Sodium Thiosulphate (Na₂S₂O₃)** and **Iodine (I₂)** is one of the most important and widely utilized redox (reduction-oxidation) reactions in analytical chemistry. It forms the foundation of **iodometry**, a powerful titrimetric method used to determine the concentration of various oxidizing and reducing agents in diverse fields, from environmental monitoring and food safety to pharmaceutical quality control and industrial process management [1].

This comprehensive article provides an in-depth exploration of the Sodium Thiosulphate and Iodine reaction. We will analyze the **reaction mechanism**, present the **balanced chemical equation**, discuss the **role of indicators**, and explore its critical **analytical applications**. For laboratory technicians, chemical engineers, and researchers, a precise understanding of this reaction is essential for achieving accurate and reliable analytical results.

## 1. The Redox Mechanism: Oxidation and Reduction

The interaction between Sodium Thiosulphate and Iodine is a classic redox reaction where electrons are transferred between the reactants. In this process, thiosulphate ions act as the reducing agent, and iodine acts as the oxidizing agent.

### 1.1. Oxidation of Thiosulphate to Tetrathionate
The thiosulphate ion (S₂O₃²⁻) is oxidized to the tetrathionate ion (S₄O₆²⁻). In this half-reaction, two thiosulphate ions lose two electrons:

`2S₂O₃²⁻(aq) → S₄O₆²⁻(aq) + 2e⁻`

The oxidation state of sulfur in thiosulphate is effectively +2, while in tetrathionate, it is +2.5. This specific oxidation to tetrathionate is unique to the reaction with iodine under neutral or slightly acidic conditions. Stronger oxidizing agents (like chlorine or bromine) would oxidize thiosulphate further to sulfate (SO₄²⁻) [2].

### 1.2. Reduction of Iodine to Iodide
Simultaneously, the iodine molecule (I₂) is reduced to two iodide ions (I⁻). In this half-reaction, the iodine molecule gains the two electrons lost by the thiosulphate ions:

`I₂(aq) + 2e⁻ → 2I⁻(aq)`

The oxidation state of iodine changes from 0 in the elemental form to -1 in the iodide ion. In aqueous solution, iodine is typically present as the triiodide ion (I₃⁻) due to the addition of potassium iodide (KI), but the fundamental redox chemistry remains the same.

## 2. The Balanced Chemical Equation

Combining the two half-reactions gives the overall balanced ionic equation for the reaction:

`2S₂O₃²⁻(aq) + I₂(aq) → S₄O₆²⁻(aq) + 2I⁻(aq)`

In terms of the full molecular equation using Sodium Thiosulphate:

`2Na₂S₂O₃(aq) + I₂(aq) → Na₂S₄O₆(aq) + 2NaI(aq)`

This equation clearly shows the 2:1 stoichiometric ratio between Sodium Thiosulphate and Iodine, which is the key to its use in quantitative analysis. Two moles of Sodium Thiosulphate are required to react completely with one mole of Iodine [3].

## 3. The Role of the Indicator: Achieving Precision

The visual detection of the endpoint in an iodine-thiosulphate titration is greatly enhanced by the use of a **starch indicator**.

*   **Mechanism**: Starch forms a deep blue-black complex with iodine (specifically with the triiodide ion, I₃⁻).
*   **Endpoint Detection**: During the titration, as Sodium Thiosulphate is added from the burette, the concentration of iodine decreases. When the last trace of iodine is reacted away, the blue-black color of the starch-iodine complex suddenly disappears, leaving a colorless solution. This sharp color change marks the endpoint of the titration with high precision.
*   **Best Practice**: Starch indicator should be added near the end of the titration, when the solution has turned a pale straw-yellow color. Adding it too early can lead to the formation of a very stable complex that reacts slowly, potentially causing an overestimation of the endpoint [4].

## 4. Critical Analytical Applications: Iodometry in Practice

The Sodium Thiosulphate-Iodine reaction is the cornerstone of iodometric titrations, which are used to quantify a vast array of substances.

### 4.1. Determination of Dissolved Oxygen (Winkler Method)
In environmental science, the Winkler method uses iodometry to measure the dissolved oxygen (DO) content in water samples. DO is a critical indicator of water quality and ecosystem health. The oxygen in the sample is used to oxidize manganese, which then reacts with iodide to release a stoichiometric amount of iodine. This iodine is then titrated with a standard Sodium Thiosulphate solution [5].

### 4.2. Analysis of Bleaching Agents and Disinfectants
Iodometry is used to determine the "available chlorine" in bleaching powders and liquid bleaches (sodium hypochlorite). The hypochlorite reacts with excess potassium iodide to release iodine, which is then titrated with thiosulphate. This ensures that products meet their specified concentrations for effective disinfection and bleaching.

### 4.3. Determination of Copper in Ores and Alloys
In metallurgical analysis, copper(II) ions can be determined by reacting them with excess iodide. This reaction produces copper(I) iodide precipitate and releases iodine. The released iodine is then titrated with Sodium Thiosulphate to calculate the original copper concentration.

### 4.4. Vitamin C (Ascorbic Acid) Quantification
Vitamin C is a strong reducing agent. Its concentration in juices, supplements, and pharmaceutical formulations can be determined by titrating it directly with a standard iodine solution (iodimetry) or by adding an excess of iodine and back-titrating the remaining iodine with Sodium Thiosulphate (iodometry).

### 4.5. Quality Control in the Food Industry
Iodometry is used to measure the peroxide value of fats and oils, which is an indicator of rancidity and oxidative stability. It is also used to determine the concentration of sulfur dioxide (used as a preservative) in wines and dried fruits.

## 5. Factors Affecting the Reaction and Accuracy

To ensure the highest accuracy in iodometric titrations, several factors must be carefully managed:

*   **pH Control**: The reaction should ideally be carried out in neutral or slightly acidic conditions (pH < 8). In strongly alkaline solutions, iodine reacts with hydroxide ions to form hypoiodite and iodate, which do not react stoichiometrically with thiosulphate.
*   **Temperature**: The titration should be performed at room temperature. Higher temperatures can lead to the loss of volatile iodine and decrease the sensitivity of the starch indicator.
*   **Air Oxidation of Iodide**: In strongly acidic solutions, iodide ions can be slowly oxidized by atmospheric oxygen to release iodine, leading to falsely high results. This is minimized by working quickly and sometimes using an inert atmosphere (e.g., CO₂).
*   **Stability of Thiosulphate Solutions**: Standard Sodium Thiosulphate solutions can be decomposed by bacteria or by atmospheric CO₂. They are typically stabilized by adding a small amount of sodium carbonate and stored in dark bottles [6].

### 5.1. Advanced Reaction Kinetics and Mechanism Deep Dive

The reaction between sodium thiosulphate and iodine is a classic example of a redox titration, but the underlying kinetics and molecular interactions are far more complex than a simple stoichiometry would suggest. In aqueous solution, the thiosulphate ion ($S_2O_3^{2-}$) acts as a soft nucleophile, attacking the electrophilic iodine molecule ($I_2$).

#### Step-by-Step Molecular Mechanism

1.  **Formation of the Intermediate Complex**: When iodine is added to a thiosulphate solution, it first forms a transient charge-transfer complex. This intermediate is often short-lived but is critical for the rapid electron transfer that follows.
2.  **Nucleophilic Attack**: The terminal sulfur atom of the thiosulphate ion, which carries a significant negative charge density, attacks one of the iodine atoms. This leads to the displacement of an iodide ion ($I^-$) and the formation of a thiosulphate-iodine intermediate ($S_2O_3I^-$).
3.  **Secondary Nucleophilic Attack**: A second thiosulphate ion then attacks the sulfur atom of the $S_2O_3I^-$ intermediate. This displace the second iodine atom as an iodide ion and results in the formation of the tetrathionate ion ($S_4O_6^{2-}$).

#### Why Tetrathionate?
Unlike stronger oxidizing agents like chlorine or bromine, which can oxidize thiosulphate all the way to sulphate ($SO_4^{2-}$), iodine is a relatively mild oxidant. Its oxidation potential is perfectly tuned to stop the reaction at the tetrathionate stage. This "gentle" oxidation is what makes the iodine-thiosulphate reaction so reliable for analytical chemistry; there are no side reactions or over-oxidation products to interfere with the results.

### 5.2. Critical Success Factors in Iodometric Titrations

To achieve the high precision required in industrial quality control (QC) laboratories, several factors must be strictly controlled:

| Factor | Impact on Accuracy | Mitigation Strategy |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **pH Level** | High pH can lead to the formation of hypoiodite ($IO^-$), which over-oxidizes thiosulphate. | Maintain pH below 7, typically in slightly acidic conditions. |
| **Light Sensitivity** | Iodine solutions decompose when exposed to strong UV light. | Use amber glassware and store in dark cabinets. |
| **Oxygen Interference** | Atmospheric oxygen can oxidize iodide back to iodine in acidic solutions. | Perform titrations promptly and minimize agitation. |
| **Indicator Timing** | Adding starch too early can trap iodine in the starch helix, leading to a slow and inaccurate endpoint. | Add starch indicator only when the solution is pale yellow. |

### 5.3. Case Study: Determining Chlorine Purity in Industrial Bleach

In a large-scale textile manufacturing facility, the incoming sodium hypochlorite (bleach) must be verified for its active chlorine content. The laboratory uses an indirect iodometric titration:

1.  **Sample Preparation**: A measured volume of bleach is added to an excess of potassium iodide (KI) in an acidic environment.
2.  **Iodine Liberation**: The hypochlorite reacts with the iodide to release a proportional amount of free iodine, turning the solution dark brown.
3.  **Titration**: This liberated iodine is then titrated against a standardized 0.1N Sodium Thiosulphate solution.
4.  **Result Calculation**: The volume of thiosulphate used is directly proportional to the active chlorine in the original bleach sample.

This method is preferred over direct titration because it is faster, more visual, and less prone to interference from other ions present in industrial-grade bleach.
