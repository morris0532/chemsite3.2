## A-Z Glossary of Soda Ash Terms

### A - Alkalinity
The total alkali content of Soda Ash, typically expressed as a percentage of **Sodium Carbonate (Na₂CO₃)**. High-purity industrial grade is usually ≥ 99.2%.

### B - Bulk Density
The weight of the Soda Ash per unit of volume (e.g., kg/m³ or g/cm³). **Dense Soda Ash** has a bulk density of 0.9 - 1.2 g/cm³, while **Light Soda Ash** is 0.5 - 0.7 g/cm³.

### C - Caking
The formation of hard lumps or a solid mass due to moisture absorption. This is a common issue for Soda Ash stored in high-humidity environments.

### D - Dense Soda Ash
A granular form of Soda Ash produced by hydrating and then dehydrating Light Soda Ash. Its larger particle size and higher density make it ideal for glass furnaces.

### E - E500(i)
The European food additive code for Sodium Carbonate. Food-grade Soda Ash must meet strict purity and heavy metal limits (e.g., Lead ≤ 2 ppm).

### F - FOB (Free on Board)
An Incoterm where the seller is responsible for the material and inland transport to the port. The buyer takes over the risk and cost once the material is loaded onto the ship.

### G - GHS (Globally Harmonized System)
The international standard for the classification and labeling of chemicals. Soda Ash is classified as an eye irritant (Category 2).

### H - Hou Process (Combined Process)
A production method developed by Chinese chemist **Hou Debang**. It produces Soda Ash and Ammonium Chloride simultaneously, offering higher resource efficiency and lower chloride levels.

### I - Iron (Fe) Content
A critical impurity for the glass industry. High-purity Soda Ash typically has an iron content of **≤ 20 ppm (0.002%)**.

### J - Jumbo Bag (FIBC)
A large, heavy-duty woven bag (typically 1000kg or 1250kg) used for bulk transport and automated unloading.

### L - Light Soda Ash
The primary form of Soda Ash produced in the Solvay or Hou process. It is a fine, white powder with a high surface area, making it ideal for rapid dissolution.

### M - Moisture Content
The amount of water present in the Soda Ash. Industrial grade typically has a moisture content of **≤ 0.2%**.

### N - Natural Soda Ash
Soda Ash produced from natural trona or sodium carbonate-rich brines (e.g., from the Green River basin in the USA or the Salt Lakes in China).

### P - PSD (Particle Size Distribution)
The range of particle sizes in a batch of Soda Ash. For glass manufacturing, a narrow and consistent PSD is essential for preventing "batch segregation."

### R - REACH Registration
The European regulation for the registration of chemicals. Soda Ash is registered under **EC Number 207-838-8**.

### S - Solvay Process (Ammonia-Soda Process)
The traditional method for producing Soda Ash from salt (brine) and limestone, using ammonia as a catalyst.

### T - TDS (Technical Data Sheet)
A document provided by the manufacturer that lists the chemical and physical specifications of the Soda Ash.

### W - Water Insoluble Matter
Trace amounts of sand, unreacted minerals, or other particles that do not dissolve in water. High-purity grade is typically **≤ 0.03%**.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

### 1. What is the difference between "Total Alkali" and "Sodium Carbonate"?
In the context of Soda Ash, they are essentially the same. "Total Alkali" is the measurement of the alkaline strength, expressed as the equivalent percentage of Na₂CO₃.

### 2. Why is "Bulk Density" so important for logistics?
Bulk density determines how much weight can fit into a 20'GP container. You can fit ~27MT of Dense Soda Ash, but only ~21MT of Light Soda Ash.

### 3. What does "Hygroscopic" mean for my warehouse?
It means the Soda Ash will "pull" moisture from the air. You must store it in a dry area and use bags with high-quality PE liners to prevent caking.

### 4. Is "Soda Ash" the same as "Baking Soda"?
No. Soda Ash is **Sodium Carbonate (Na₂CO₃)**, a strong alkali. Baking Soda is **Sodium Bicarbonate (NaHCO₃)**, a much milder alkali used in food and personal care.

### 5. How do I verify the "PSD" of a shipment?
The PSD is measured using a "Sieve Analysis" test. The results are typically listed in the Certificate of Analysis (COA) as the percentage of material retained on specific mesh sizes.
