## 1. Initial Physical Inspection
Before heading to the lab, a simple physical check can reveal a lot about the material's quality and handling history.
*   **Color and Clarity**: High-purity Borax should be a brilliant white, crystalline powder. Any gray, yellow, or brown tints indicate contamination (often iron or organic matter).
*   **Flowability and Caking**: The sample should flow freely. The presence of hard lumps (caking) suggests that the material has been exposed to moisture or stored under excessive pressure.
*   **Odor**: Borax is naturally odorless. Any chemical or "musty" smell indicates contamination or improper storage.

---

## 2. Laboratory Chemical Analysis
The core of the evaluation is verifying the chemical specifications against the provided Certificate of Analysis (COA).
*   **B₂O₃ Content**: This is the primary measure of the Borax's strength. Verify that the B₂O₃ percentage matches the grade you require (e.g., ~36.5% for Decahydrate, ~48.8% for Pentahydrate).
*   **Purity (Sodium Tetraborate Content)**: Ensure the purity is typically >99.5% for technical grades.
*   **Impurity Profile**:
    *   **Iron (Fe)**: Critical for glass and ceramics; should typically be <20 ppm.
    *   **Chlorides (Cl) and Sulfates (SO₄)**: High levels can cause corrosion in metalworking or affect the clarity of glass.
    *   **Insolubles**: Measure the amount of material that does not dissolve in water; high insolubles can clog filters and nozzles in industrial spray systems.

---

## 3. Physical Property Testing
*   **Particle Size Distribution (Sieve Analysis)**: Ensure the mesh size matches your process requirements. Material that is too fine can cause dusting issues, while material that is too coarse may not dissolve quickly enough.
*   **Solubility Rate**: Measure how quickly the sample dissolves in water at your process temperature. This is vital for ensuring consistent batch times in manufacturing.
*   **Bulk Density**: Important for calibrating your dosing and feeding equipment.

---

## 4. Pilot-Scale and Application Testing
The final proof of quality is how the material performs in your actual application.
*   **Small-Batch Production**: Run a small test batch using the sample to observe its behavior in your specific formulation (e.g., its effect on glass viscosity or detergent pH stability).
*   **Compatibility Testing**: Ensure the Borax does not react negatively with other raw materials in your process.

---

## 5. Evaluating the Supplier's Documentation
A sample is only as good as the data that accompanies it.
*   **Batch Traceability**: Does the sample label include a batch number that matches the provided COA?
*   **Technical Data Sheet (TDS) and SDS**: Are the provided documents up-to-date, accurate, and compliant with your regional regulations?
