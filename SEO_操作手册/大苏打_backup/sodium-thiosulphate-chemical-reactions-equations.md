The versatility of **Sodium Thiosulphate (Na₂S₂O₃)** in industrial and laboratory applications is a direct result of its unique chemical reactivity. As a moderate reducing agent and a strong complexing agent, it participates in a wide range of reactions that are fundamental to analytical chemistry, water treatment, and metallurgy. This guide provides a technical deep dive into the most important chemical reactions of Sodium Thiosulphate, complete with balanced equations and their practical significance.

## 1. Reaction with Strong Acids (The "Disappearing Cross" Experiment)

One of the most classic reactions in chemistry education and kinetics is the reaction between Sodium Thiosulphate and a strong acid, such as hydrochloric acid (HCl).

**The Balanced Equation:**
**Na₂S₂O₃(aq) + 2HCl(aq) → 2NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + SO₂(g) + S(s)↓**

*   **Observation**: The solution gradually becomes cloudy (turbid) due to the formation of a fine yellow precipitate of elemental sulfur.
*   **Significance**: This reaction is widely used to study the effect of concentration and temperature on the rate of reaction. The sulfur dioxide (SO₂) gas produced has a pungent odor and must be handled in a ventilated area.

---

## 2. Reaction with Iodine (Iodometry)

The quantitative reaction between Sodium Thiosulphate and iodine is the foundation of iodometric titrations, a cornerstone of analytical chemistry.

**The Balanced Equation:**
**2Na₂S₂O₃(aq) + I₂(aq) → Na₂S₄O₆(aq) + 2NaI(aq)**
*(Ionic form: 2S₂O₃²⁻ + I₂ → S₄O₆²⁻ + 2I⁻)*

*   **Mechanism**: The thiosulphate ion (S₂O₃²⁻) is oxidized to the tetrathionate ion (S₄O₆²⁻), while iodine (I₂) is reduced to iodide ions (I⁻).
*   **Significance**: This reaction is highly specific and proceeds rapidly to completion, allowing for the precise determination of oxidizing agents in various samples.

---

## 3. Dechlorination Reaction

In water treatment, Sodium Thiosulphate is used to neutralize free chlorine (hypochlorous acid).

**The Balanced Equation:**
**Na₂S₂O₃ + 4HOCl + H₂O → 2NaHSO₄ + 4HCl**

*   **Mechanism**: Thiosulphate reduces the highly reactive hypochlorite/chlorine into harmless chloride ions.
*   **Significance**: This reaction is nearly instantaneous and is essential for protecting aquatic life from the toxic effects of residual chlorine in treated water.

---

## 4. Complexation with Silver Halides (Photography)

The ability of thiosulphate to form stable, water-soluble complexes with silver ions is the basis of its use as a photographic "fixer."

**The Balanced Equation:**
**AgBr(s) + 2Na₂S₂O₃(aq) → Na₃[Ag(S₂O₃)₂](aq) + NaBr(aq)**

*   **Mechanism**: The thiosulphate ions react with the solid, unexposed silver bromide (AgBr) on the film to form the soluble dithiosulphatoargentate(I) complex.
*   **Significance**: This process "fixes" the image by removing the light-sensitive silver halides, preventing the film from further darkening when exposed to light.

---

## 5. Thermal Decomposition

When heated, Sodium Thiosulphate Pentahydrate undergoes a series of changes, eventually decomposing at high temperatures.

**The Reaction (at high heat):**
**4Na₂S₂O₃ → 3Na₂SO₄ + Na₂S₅**

*   **Mechanism**: At temperatures above 223°C, it decomposes into sodium sulfate and sodium pentasulfide.
*   **Significance**: Understanding thermal stability is critical for industrial processes involving high-temperature drying or chemical synthesis.
