In the field of industrial water treatment and chemical processing, **Sodium Thiosulphate (Na₂S₂O₃)** and **Sodium Bisulfite (NaHSO₃)** are two of the most widely used reducing agents. While they are often used for the same primary purpose—dechlorination—they have distinct chemical properties, reaction mechanisms, and environmental profiles. Choosing the wrong one can lead to operational inefficiencies, increased costs, or unintended environmental impacts. This guide provides a detailed technical comparison to help you make an informed selection for your specific application.

## 1. Chemical Composition and Physical Properties

The fundamental differences between these two chemicals start with their chemical structure and the form in which they are typically supplied.

| Property | Sodium Thiosulphate | Sodium Bisulfite |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Chemical Formula** | Na₂S₂O₃·5H₂O (Pentahydrate) | NaHSO₃ |
| **Physical Form** | Clear, colorless crystals (Solid) | Clear, colorless to pale yellow liquid |
| **Molecular Weight** | 248.18 g/mol | 104.06 g/mol |
| **Solubility** | Highly soluble in water | Extremely soluble (usually 38-40% solution) |
| **pH (Solution)** | 6.5 – 8.5 (Nearly neutral) | 3.5 – 5.0 (Acidic) |
| **Odor** | Odorless | Pungent, sulfurous odor (SO₂) |

## 2. Dechlorination: Mechanism and Efficiency

Both chemicals are highly effective at removing free chlorine, but they react differently and have different stoichiometric requirements.

### Sodium Thiosulphate (The "Eco-Friendly" Choice):
*   **Reaction**: Na₂S₂O₃ + 4HOCl + H₂O → 2NaHSO₄ + 4HCl
*   **Advantages**: It is non-toxic to aquatic life and does not significantly deplete dissolved oxygen (DO) levels in the water. It is the preferred choice for aquariums, aquaculture, and sensitive environmental discharge.
*   **Dosage**: Requires approximately 1.6 to 2.2 parts of thiosulphate to neutralize 1 part of chlorine.

### Sodium Bisulfite (The "Cost-Effective" Choice):
*   **Reaction**: NaHSO₃ + HOCl → NaHSO₄ + HCl
*   **Advantages**: It is a very fast-acting dechlorinator and is often more cost-effective for large-scale industrial wastewater treatment due to its lower molecular weight and liquid form, which is easy to meter.
*   **Disadvantages**: It is a strong oxygen scavenger. Over-dosing can significantly deplete dissolved oxygen levels, which can be harmful to fish. It also lowers the pH of the water more than thiosulphate.
*   **Dosage**: Requires approximately 1.4 to 1.6 parts of bisulfite to neutralize 1 part of chlorine.

## 3. Key Application Differences

While they overlap in water treatment, their other applications are quite distinct:

*   **Photography**: Only **Sodium Thiosulphate** is used as a "fixer" to dissolve unexposed silver halides. Bisulfite is sometimes used as a preservative in developers but cannot act as a fixing agent.
*   **Food Industry**: **Sodium Bisulfite** is widely used as a preservative and antioxidant in the food and beverage industry (e.g., in wine and dried fruits). Sodium Thiosulphate has very limited food-grade applications.
*   **Pulp and Paper**: **Sodium Bisulfite** is used extensively in the pulping process and for bleaching mechanical pulp. Sodium Thiosulphate is primarily used as an "antichlor" after the bleaching stage.
*   **Gold Mining**: **Sodium Thiosulphate** is used as a non-toxic leaching agent. Bisulfite is primarily used in the destruction of cyanide in traditional mining tailings.

## 4. Safety and Handling Considerations

*   **Odor and Inhalation**: Sodium Bisulfite solutions release sulfur dioxide (SO₂) gas, which is irritating to the respiratory system. It must be handled in well-ventilated areas. Sodium Thiosulphate is odorless and much more pleasant to handle.
*   **Corrosivity**: Due to its acidic nature, Sodium Bisulfite solutions are more corrosive to stainless steel and other metals than Sodium Thiosulphate solutions.
*   **Storage**: Bisulfite solutions are sensitive to air and will slowly oxidize to sulfate, losing their strength. They must be stored in tightly sealed, UV-protected containers. Sodium Thiosulphate crystals are very stable if kept dry.

---

## 5. Selection Guide: Which One Should You Choose?

**Choose Sodium Thiosulphate if:**
*   You are treating water for aquariums, aquaculture, or sensitive natural discharge.
*   You want an odorless, easy-to-handle solid chemical.
*   You need to maintain stable dissolved oxygen and pH levels.
*   Your application is in photography or specialized gold leaching.

**Choose Sodium Bisulfite if:**
*   You are performing large-scale industrial wastewater treatment where cost and liquid metering are primary factors.
*   You have robust ventilation and pH control systems in place.
*   You are looking for a food-grade preservative or antioxidant.
*   You are involved in the pulp and paper manufacturing process.
